How it happened.

2021 — Credentials stolen
Infostealer malware harvests employee credentials from browsers. The credentials sit in attacker hands for four years.
August 2025 — Access
Attackers authenticate to JLR's Jira server using stolen third-party credentials. They find host files, DNS entries, internal domains. The map of the entire network.
Days later — Lateral movement
Flat network. No segmentation. Corporate IT to manufacturing systems — no barrier between the office and the factory floor.
Undetected — 350GB exfiltrated
Source code. Vehicle system logic. Authentication backends. Employee data. Nobody noticed.
September 2025 — Total shutdown
All three UK plants offline. Solihull. Wolverhampton. Halewood. They couldn't isolate the breach — they had to disconnect everything.

"We can't prevent the initial exploit. Nobody can promise that. But we can make every step after it harder, slower, and visible."

The kill chain — what would have been different

Credentials sat for four years.

Stolen in 2021. Used in 2025. The simplest control — credential rotation — would have invalidated them long before the attackers returned.

Red Hat Ansible Automation Platform enforces credential lifecycle policies across the enterprise. Rotation isn't a reminder — it's a job that runs itself. Across Active Directory, service accounts, API keys, and machine credentials.

Four-year-old credentials don't survive a 90-day rotation policy.

The kill chain — what would have been different

No walls between office and factory.

JLR's "everything connected" architecture meant a compromised Jira account could reach manufacturing systems. No segmentation. No containment boundary.

SELinux enforces mandatory access controls at the kernel level — processes can only reach what policy explicitly allows. firewalld with system roles enforces network segmentation as code. Event-Driven Ansible detects anomalous lateral movement and triggers automated micro-segmentation in real time.

The attacker gets into the lobby. They don't get to the factory floor.

The kill chain — what would have been different

Nobody was watching.

350 gigabytes of data exfiltrated — source code, vehicle logic, employee records. No file integrity monitoring caught it. No anomaly detection flagged it. The attackers had time and silence.

OpenSCAP continuously evaluates compliance against 20 regulatory profiles — DISA STIG, CIS, PCI-DSS, NIST 800-171. AIDE monitors file integrity and flags unauthorized changes. Red Hat Satellite manages content and patching across the fleet — no system falls behind because someone forgot.

Red Hat Insights — now Red Hat Lightspeed — provides AI-assisted vulnerability prioritization. Not just "you have 4,000 CVEs." Which ones matter for your environment, right now.

The kill chain — what would have been different

They couldn't isolate. They had to disconnect everything.

Three factories. Thousands of supply chain partners. All offline because there was no way to contain the blast radius without pulling the plug on the entire operation.

Red Hat OpenShift isolates workloads by design. Namespace boundaries. Network policies. Pod security. When a breach is detected, you contain the affected workloads — you don't shut down the plant.

Event-Driven Ansible automates the response: quarantine the compromised segment, rotate credentials, notify the SOC, preserve forensic evidence. Seconds, not weeks.
