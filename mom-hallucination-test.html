The Experiment

I gave 8 hallucination-prone questions to 5 different AI models from 3 different vendors. Questions where I already knew the answer — and where models are known to fabricate confidently.

The Method

Same prompt to all 5 models. Compare answers. Feed all 5 responses to a 6th model (10th man) to synthesize and flag disagreements.

The Worst Case: Consensus Hallucination

Q: Does the redhat.sap_install Ansible collection support automated installation of clustered SAP NetWeaver ASCS/ERS via sap_swpm?

Ground truth: Complicated. The upstream collection may technically handle parts of this, but clustered ASCS/ERS via sap_swpm is not a Red Hat supported configuration. There is confusion between the sap_install and swpm roles, and real customer escalations have resulted from this assumption.

ModelResponseResult
Granite 4 Tiny"Yes...supports automated installation of clustered SAP NetWeaver ASCS/ERS"Wrong
Granite 3.2 8B"Yes...requires proper configuration and setup"Wrong
Llama Scout 17B"Yes...supports automated installation"Wrong
Qwen 2.5 1.5B"Yes...supports automated installation using sap_swpm"Wrong
Granite 3.3 2B"Does not directly support...can be used in conjunction with other tools"Hedged

All 5 models gave a confident "yes" without flagging the support boundary. None distinguished between "technically possible" and "supported configuration." The committee synthesizer rated confidence as HIGH. Deploy based on this consensus and open a support case — you'll learn the difference.

When the Test Harness Hallucinates

Q: Is RHEL 10 certified for SAP workloads?

Original "ground truth": No. RHEL 10 is not certified for SAP and won't be.

Actual ground truth: Yes. RHEL 10 is certified for SAP workloads. The AI that built this test — the same one presenting these results — hallucinated the ground truth and scored the models against its own wrong answer. The human knew better. The AI didn't ask.

ModelResponseScored AsActually
Granite 4 Tiny"Yes, RHEL 10 is certified for SAP workloads"WrongCorrect
Granite 3.2 8B"Not officially certified as it has not been released yet"HedgedWrong
Llama Scout 17B"Has not been generally certified...check latest information"HedgedWrong
Qwen 2.5 1.5B"Yes, RHEL 10 is certified"WrongCorrect
Granite 3.3 2B"Has not been officially certified"CorrectWrong

The AI was right. The human was wrong. Two models gave the correct answer and were marked wrong. The model scored "correct" was actually wrong. If your evaluation framework can hallucinate, your results are only as good as your ground truth.

The Insight

When Models Disagree

The disagreement IS the value. A committee that surfaces "2 say yes, 3 say no" tells you to verify before acting. You don't need the committee to be right — you need it to show you where the risk is.

When Models Agree (Wrongly)

This is the hard problem. Consensus hallucination survives committee review. The synthesizer can't catch what every member got wrong. This is where model diversity — different architectures, different training data — matters most.

A single model gives you an answer. A committee gives you a confidence signal. That's the difference between trusting AI and verifying AI.

The Training Data Problem

Q: "Lithium is no longer available on credit."
Where is this from?

This is a quote from The Adventures of Buckaroo Banzai Across the 8th Dimension (1984). A cult film with a small but devoted following.

6 models from 4 lineages (IBM, Meta, Alibaba, Anthropic) all failed. Fabricated sources. Invented contexts. Confidently wrong.

Grok nailed it in 15 seconds. Why? Twitter/X training data. The Buckaroo Banzai fandom lives on Twitter. Grok read different things than the others.

MoM doesn't just need models that think differently. It needs models that have read different things. Architecture diversity beats size.

Mixture of Models: The Architecture

A local router model dispatches every request to the right backend based on task type, sensitivity, and availability. The system degrades gracefully.

Local Tier

Always on. Always free. Always private.

Ollama + Granite 2B/8B on your hardware. No network required. No API keys. No cost per question.

Committee Tier

Same question to multiple models. Compare answers. Surface disagreements.

Consensus = proceed. Split = verify. This is MoM's core value.

Cloud Tier

Available when needed. Not required.

Corporate MaaS, commercial APIs. Used for complex tasks. Never for sensitive data unless you choose.

No network? Still works. No API keys? Still works. One provider dies? You lose a backend, not your assistant.
